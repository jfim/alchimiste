# Autoresearch roadmap — 2026-05-25

A snapshot. This document maps the *research questions* we want to
answer in the next few sessions. The experiments are small and
self-contained enough that they don't need individual specs or PRs
— just run them directly (Hydra overrides via `just train-seeds`)
and record results in [log.md](log.md). If we notice afterwards
that we ran something wrong, rerunning is cheap.

Expect this file to be obsoleted within days as results come in —
supersede it with a freshly-dated roadmap rather than editing in
place.

## Why now

The infrastructure batch that landed late 2026-05-22
([#10](https://github.com/jfim/alchimiste/pull/10)–[#18](https://github.com/jfim/alchimiste/pull/18))
turned the training loop from "single-run-noisy-fluke generator" into
"defensible seed-averaged sweeps." Every claim from batches 1–2 in
[log.md](log.md) is suspect under the new infra (no `torch.manual_seed`
before [#14](https://github.com/jfim/alchimiste/pull/14)). The roadmap
below is the disciplined re-establishment of what we actually know,
plus the open questions that batches 1–2 *couldn't* answer because the
noise floor was ±0.10–0.25 F1.

With seeding fixed, expected within-seed std on test F1 is ~0.01
(same `seed`, same `data.split_seed`, repeated). The remaining
variance comes from two independent sources we now control
separately: training-init variance (vary `seed`, hold
`data.split_seed`) and split variance (vary `data.split_seed`,
hold `seed`). Pre-fix [log.md](log.md) saw cross-seed std ≈ 0.25
with both varying and no seeding — under the new infra we don't yet
know the init-only std, but the dominant share of pre-fix variance
was missing seeding, so init-only std is expected to be small
(rough guess ~0.02–0.05; **establishing this number is part of Q1**).

Default for every sweep below: hold `data.split_seed` constant,
vary training `seed`. Seed count is tuned to cost:

- **distilbert-only sweeps (Q1, Q3, Q4): 10 seeds.** Each run is
  1–2 min on a 3060; the `train-seeds` harness ([#15](https://github.com/jfim/alchimiste/pull/15))
  amortizes startup across all seeds in one process. SEM scales as
  std/√n, so 10 seeds buys ~40% tighter intervals than 5 for
  ~negligible extra wall-clock.
- **Slow-arch sweeps (Q2 ModernBERT/NeoBERT): 5 seeds.** Each run
  is ~25 min, so 5-seed sweeps are ~2h apiece — comfortably the
  practical ceiling. SEM ≈ std/√5 ≈ 0.022 if std lands at ~0.05.

Bump higher if a measured std turns out wider than expected.

## The core question

**Which lever has the biggest effect on the metric: backbone, head,
or freeze/thaw?** Each of Q1–Q4 tests one lever in isolation on a
cheap baseline so the four together produce a ranked recommendation
matrix. When more labels land later, that ranking tells us which
direction to invest in first.

Concretely: if Q2 shows ModernBERT cleanly beats distilbert, the
post-label work focuses on getting ModernBERT to scale with data.
If Q3 shows a non-linear head is the win, the post-label work
focuses on head architecture. If Q4 shows freeze is within noise,
the post-label iteration story becomes much cheaper. And if
distilbert is competitive across the board, that's a fine outcome
too — we ship the cheap model.

To keep total compute honest, Q3 and Q4 (which sweep many variants)
run only on distilbert@512. Cross-architecture sanity checks
("does the Q3 winner also win on ModernBERT?") are deferred to the
*next* roadmap, conditional on each lever showing a real effect here.

## The four research questions

| # | Question | Why we care | Sweep shape (rough) |
|---|----------|-------------|----------------------|
| Q1 | Does the cheapest setup (distilbert@512, fine-tuned, default linear head) actually work above the noise floor? | Establishes the new baseline. Until this is known, *every* other result needs to be compared against air. | 1 config × 10 seeds |
| Q2 | **Backbone lever.** How much does encoder complexity move the metric? distilbert@512 → ModernBERT-base@8192 → NeoBERT-base@? | Tells us whether the task is simple enough that the small/fast model is fine, or complex enough to need real backbone capacity. Big implication for inference cost, training cost, and VRAM. | 3 archs × 5 seeds (distilbert reuses Q1's 10; ModernBERT/NeoBERT 5 each at the context length they can fit) |
| Q3 | **Head lever.** Which head architectures move the metric — and is the answer **decent or catastrophic**? Variants: linear (baseline), linear + positional features, Conv1D, BiLSTM, CRF, BiLSTM+CRF. | On 141 train articles, parameter-heavy heads may be untrainable (CRF transition matrix needs data; BiLSTM adds capacity that overfits). Knowing *which* head changes survive small data — and which collapse — tells us where the head-side ceiling is. | distilbert@512 × 6 head variants × 10 seeds (~60 runs, ~1–2h total at 1–2 min/run; linear baseline reuses Q1) |
| Q4 | **Freeze/thaw lever.** Does freezing the backbone hurt, help, or land within noise? | Frozen encoders need less data, train faster, overfit less. If "frozen" is within noise of "fine-tuned," iteration gets dramatically cheaper and the head becomes *the* place to invest. If frozen is catastrophic, the encoder is doing real task-specific work. | distilbert@512 × {frozen / fine-tuned} × 10 seeds, linear head only (~10 new runs; fine-tuned reuses Q1) |

## Why this ordering

Each lever is tested independently, on the cheapest plausible
configuration, so the four results can be ranked side-by-side at the
end. No mid-roadmap conditioning — every lever runs.

1. **Q1 + Q2 together** — same harness (varying `model=` and
   `data.max_seq_len`), shared seed grid. Output: trustworthy
   baselines for the three architectures with mean ± std, plus a
   cost-vs-quality plot (train time, VRAM, inference latency × test
   F1). The distilbert@512 numbers from Q1 are reused in Q3 and Q4
   as the baseline-head and fine-tuned arms.
2. **Q3** — head architecture ablation on distilbert@512. 6
   variants × 10 seeds = 60 runs, all cheap (~1–2 min/run).
   Deliverable is a bar chart ranked by mean test F1 with error
   bars, answering the "decent or catastrophic" framing directly.
3. **Q4** — freeze vs fine-tune on distilbert@512 with the linear
   head. 10 new runs (the fine-tuned arm is Q1; only the frozen
   arm is new). Isolates the freeze effect from the head effect.

The combined output is a single table:

| Lever | Best variant | Δ test-F1 vs baseline | Cost note |
|-------|--------------|------------------------|-----------|
| Backbone | (Q2 winner) | (Δ) | (train/infer cost) |
| Head | (Q3 winner) | (Δ) | (params added) |
| Freeze/thaw | freeze or thaw | (Δ) | (train-cost ratio) |

Whichever row has the biggest |Δ| above the combined SEM is the
lever to prioritize once more labels land. Crossings (head × freeze,
head on a bigger backbone, etc.) are deferred to the next roadmap
and depend on which levers show real effects here.

Note on dropped Q5: the learning-curve experiment is omitted because
the user's prior is already "more data is needed." If a later result
genuinely contradicts that prior (e.g. Q3 finds that head capacity
*does* help — implying we haven't saturated the data signal), the
next roadmap can put it back.

## Notes per question

### Q1 — distilbert baseline

- Config: `model=encoder_hf` (distilbert-base-uncased), `data.max_seq_len=512`,
  `epochs=6`, `class_weight_drop=5`, defaults otherwise. Same as Run #2
  in [log.md](log.md) but with the seeding fix in place.
- 10 training seeds, fixed `data.split_seed`.
- Output: `(mean, std)` for val-precision, val-recall, val-F1,
  test-F1 @ IoU=0.5, plus the threshold-fallback rate.
- Secondary output: the init-only std measured here is the noise
  floor every subsequent question is compared against. If it turns
  out to be wide (≥0.10) even post-#14, that itself changes the
  plan — we'd need more seeds per config and the deeper questions
  get more expensive.
- Success criterion: we have a number we can quote with a defensible
  ± and use as the "must beat" bar for everything else.

### Q2 — encoder complexity ladder

- Three architectures, each at the largest context that comfortably
  fits on the RTX 3060 with bf16 + grad_checkpointing:
  - **distilbert-base-uncased @ 512** — already configured.
  - **ModernBERT-base @ 8192** — already verified to fit
    (bf16 + grad_ckpt + bs=2 + grad_accum=4); ~20–25 min/run.
  - **NeoBERT-base @ ???** — new config required
    (`configs/model/neobert.yaml`). Need to confirm: does NeoBERT
    support extended context similar to ModernBERT? What's the max
    that fits at 12 GB? Spec the config + an OOM-probe run before
    committing to a full sweep.
- The comparison is **architecture + context length jointly**, not
  architecture alone. This is the honest framing — the user's actual
  decision is "which {model, context} bundle do I want to deploy,"
  not a counterfactual "what if distilbert had 8192 context."
- Cost axis is part of the deliverable: report training time, VRAM
  peak, inference latency at the chosen context per architecture,
  alongside the quality metric. The right answer might be
  "ModernBERT@8192 beats distilbert@512 by 0.04 F1 but takes 20× the
  compute, so distilbert wins for any iterative loop."
- One open question: does NeoBERT have published numbers that suggest
  it's *meaningfully* different from ModernBERT-base on classification,
  or is it primarily a pretraining recipe difference? Worth 10 minutes
  of literature check before allocating sweep budget. If the expected
  delta is small, downscope Q2 to {distilbert, ModernBERT} and treat
  NeoBERT as a follow-up.

### Q3 — head architecture ablation

The question: on 141 train articles, which head variants train
usefully, which collapse, and how big is the spread? The honest
prior is that capacity-heavy heads (BiLSTM, CRF) need more data
than we have, so the most likely outcome is "linear and
linear+pos are decent, everything else is catastrophic" — which
is itself a load-bearing finding for where to invest next.

Variants (all on distilbert@512, fixed `data.split_seed`,
10 training seeds each):

| Variant | What it adds | Predicted small-data behavior |
|---------|--------------|--------------------------------|
| `linear` | Baseline. `Linear(hidden, num_labels)`. | The bar to beat. |
| `linear + pos` | `Linear(hidden + 2, num_labels)`; concat per-token `normalized_position_in_seq` and chunk-level `is_doc_end_visible`. `is_doc_start` is constant-true under truncation and is dropped. | Cheapest possible change; might help under truncation, neutral or noise on ModernBERT@8192. |
| `conv1d` | 1-D convolution (small kernel, e.g. 3) over `hidden` before the linear. Adds local context the encoder already has, but exposes it to the head directly. | Modest parameter count; plausible win or no-op. |
| `bilstm` | BiLSTM(hidden, hidden/2) → linear. ~4× more head parameters than linear. | Likely overfits at 141 articles. |
| `crf` | Linear emission + CRF layer with learned transition matrix. Loss + decode owned by the head. | Transition matrix (2×2 + start/end ≈ 8 params) is small; *but* with ~3 reachable test ranges, the structured-prediction win may not be observable. |
| `bilstm + crf` | Classic NER stack. | Most parameters; most likely to be catastrophic on 141 articles. |

- Run only on distilbert@512 — cheap per run, lots of variants. If
  a non-baseline head wins clearly here, the *next* roadmap can
  test whether it also wins on ModernBERT. Cross-arch checks are
  out of scope for this roadmap.
- Implementation: `heads.py` already documents this as the
  pluggability boundary; `build_head` is the registration point.
  Each new head subclass is self-contained. `crf` will need a
  dependency (e.g. `torchcrf` or a from-scratch implementation —
  pick whichever is faster to wire up when we get there).
- Data plumbing for `linear + pos` is small: `is_doc_end_visible`
  is derivable at batch time from `len(input_ids) < max_seq_len`;
  `normalized_position_in_seq` is `arange(T) / T`. No loader changes.
- Plan ablation up front for `linear + pos`: train with features,
  then evaluate with features zeroed to see if the head actually
  depends on them or just ignores them.
- Deliverable: bar chart of mean test-F1 with error bars across
  the 6 variants, plus a one-paragraph verdict on which family of
  changes survives the small-data regime.

### Q4 — frozen backbone

- One knob: don't backprop into the encoder. Train only the head.
- Compared to fine-tuning, expect: faster training (smaller backward
  pass), much less VRAM (no encoder grads), less overfitting risk on
  141 articles.
- Run only on distilbert@512 with the linear head. 2 conditions
  (frozen / fine-tuned) × 10 seeds; the fine-tuned arm is reused
  from Q1, so only 10 new runs (the frozen arm).
- "Good outcome" framing: *underperforming but close* is a win. If
  frozen is within seed variance of fine-tuned, that's a big
  simplification — frozen unlocks much cheaper iteration, and the
  head becomes *the* place to invest. If frozen is catastrophic,
  the encoder fine-tune is doing real work that no head can replace.
- The head × freeze crossing (does the Q3 winner do even better
  under freeze?) is deferred to the next roadmap. It's only worth
  running if both Q3 and Q4 individually show real effects.

## Out of scope for this roadmap

- **Label generation** (LLM-pseudo-labeling, paid labeling, etc.).
  The user's prior is that more data is needed; that work is its own
  multi-week thread and belongs in a separate roadmap. The four
  questions here are about characterizing *what we can do with the
  current corpus* so the label-generation work knows what to aim for.
- **Learning-curve / subsample experiments** (the dropped Q5).
  Omitted because more data is already the prior; revisit if Q3
  surprises us by showing capacity *does* help.
- **Loss-function experiments** (focal, asymmetric, label smoothing).
  Orthogonal to the questions here; could be a fifth bucket if any
  of Q1–Q4 surfaces calibration / precision-floor problems that look
  loss-shaped rather than head/encoder/data-shaped.
- **Multi-task or auxiliary-loss setups.**
- **Sliding-window chunking.** Only worth building if there's
  evidence the truncation is the binding constraint on quality.
  The current "model still works under truncation" hypothesis
  hasn't been tested cleanly yet (will be a side-effect of
  Q1+Q2+Q3); if results say truncation is binding, chunking goes
  in the next roadmap.

## Per-question deliverable shape

Each question, when its sweep finishes, gets a log.md entry with:

1. One-line hypothesis (what we expected).
2. Full table of seeds × metrics (val P/R/F1, test F1 @ IoU=0.5,
   threshold, fell-back flag).
3. Mean ± std (or SEM) for the headline metric.
4. Comparison against the relevant baseline with the question
   "does the delta exceed the combined SEMs?"
5. Verdict: kept / discarded / inconclusive.
6. Implication for the next question in the sequence.

## What changes between this roadmap and the next one

Replace, don't edit. When Q1/Q2 land, write
`roadmap-YYYY-MM-DD.md` reflecting the new state of the world (what
we now know, what the next priorities are, which downstream questions
got reordered or dropped). Keep the old roadmaps for archaeology;
don't try to maintain a single living document. Each roadmap is a
snapshot of "what I'd do next if I sat down now."
